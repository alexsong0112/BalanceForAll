Gender Inequality in Sports

What is Gender Inequality in Sports? How Does it Affect Athletes?

Sports should be fair for everyone, but gender inequality still exists in many areas such as pay, funding, media coverage, and leadership opportunities.

What Is It?

Gender inequality in sports refers to barriers that prevent athletes from reaching their full potential because of their gender. This leads to unequal treatment in pay, sponsorships, funding, media attention, and leadership roles. Female athletes are often the most affected, even when they perform at the same level as men. Let's look into the statistics, how's, and why's.

Pay Gap Parity

In athletics, women tend to receive significantly less pay compared to men the same sport. For example, men get up to 100x more salary than women in some extreme cases. In basketball, men earn around $13 million annually, but women earn around $580,000.

Megan Rapinoe

Was a leading voice in the US Women's National Team in advocating for the equality of men's and women's pay in soccer. She filed lawsuits in 2019 due to the discrimination as the women's team were more successful and profitable than the mens. The lawsuit led to the agreement of a 50-50 split between mens and women's pay and prize money, and $22 million paid back to the women's team.

Billie Jean King

Revolutionized sport, specifically Tennis, by threatening to boycott after winning the 1972 US Open and receiving less prize money than the men's champion. This led to the US Open being the first Grand Slam to offer equal pay for men and women due to her efforts. She then founded the Women's Tennis Association with other female tennis players, providing a powerful and collective voice for women's rights in sports. Many other Grand Slams now follow equal pay.

Media Coverage

In news reports, broadcasts, and on social media, only about 15% of sports media coverage is on women. This lack of media coverage leads to fewer fans as fewer people watch their competition. This in turn can lead to less money and sponsorships, and overall, lower salaries. This cycle of inequality is why women's sports are underappreciated.

The International Olympic Committee (IOC)

The International Olympic Committee (IOC) has begun prioritizing 50-50 male and female media attention to combat gender-biased sports coverage. In 2018, they released guidelines to ensure media coverage focuses on sporting achievements for both genders, and that female media coverage is not focused on stereotypes, appearance, or personal life. They have also created clear policies emphasizing on biased-free representation of women's sports.

UN Women

An organization that works to promote gender equality in sports media, and partners with sports bodies to improve how women athletes are portrayed in the media. Their impact has led to 3 times the global coverage of women's sports over recent years, rising about 16%. This has led to more wide-spread engagement in female athletics, resulting in better pay for women as well.

Leadership & Decision-Making Roles

Women only hold about 10% of leadership roles in global sports organizations. This means that around more than 80% of the sports governing board positions are held by men in many international federations. Even in women's sports, key decisions are often still made by males. Globally, less than 20% of elite coaching roles are held by women. Why does this matter? Well leadership roles can control budget distribution between men and women's sports funding, media coverage decisions, facility access, and prize money and tournament investment.

Kristy Coventry

A former Olympic swimmer from Zimbabwe, now the first woman elected as President of the International Olympic Committee as of 2025. Although she previously served on the IOC Executive Board and chaired key commissions, she is now one of the most powerful decision-makers in world sport. She represents a major shift in global sports governance leadership.

Fatma Samoura

She is the first female Secretary General of the Fédération Internationale de Football Association (FIFA) in 2016. She is now leading one of the most powerful sports organizations in the world, and played a key role in expanding investment and visibility in women's football. She also helped increase focus on development of the FIFA Women's World Cup.

So What?

Gender inequality in sports is still present in pay, media coverage, funding, and leadership. While progress has been made, women athletes continue to face barriers that limit visibility and opportunity. Recognizing these issues is the first step toward creating a fairer future in sport.

The game is not over yet — change is still possible.
